Section 01 — Context & Urgency

Why Action Is Required Now

Invest International occupies a structurally distinct position within the Dutch financial ecosystem. As a development finance institution operating by mandate in markets where commercial risk appetite does not reach, it faces compliance obligations technically equivalent to those of commercial banks — yet operationally far more complex. Counterparties routinely include sovereign entities, state-owned enterprises, and project vehicles in jurisdictions with limited UBO registry infrastructure. The transition from WWFT to AMLR is not a housekeeping exercise. It is a fundamental regulatory shift with a hard statutory deadline — and a supervisory community that has clearly signalled it intends to enforce it.

AFM / DNB supervisory posture: Both the AFM and DNB operate on a well-established supervisory principle: scrutiny is always proportional to risk. Institutions with the most complex risk profiles — and DFIs sit at the top of that hierarchy — receive the most intensive oversight. DNB has consistently communicated to the market that AMLR readiness is not optional and that the deadline of 10 July 2027 is absolute. Invest International, given its mandate, counterparty universe, and geographic reach, should expect to be among the institutions subject to early supervisory attention. A framework that is still being built at the deadline is not a compliant framework.

01
Hard Statutory Deadline — 10 July 2027
EU Regulation 2024/1624 (AMLR) takes direct effect on 10 July 2027, superseding WWFT entirely. There is no transitional grace period. From that date, Invest International must be demonstrably compliant across CDD, EDD, UBO verification, PEP treatment, transaction monitoring, and the full suite of AMLR obligations — not working towards compliance.
02
DFI Risk Profile — Highest Supervisory Attention
DNB and AFM prioritise supervision on institutions with the greatest inherent risk exposure. Invest International's mandate makes this unavoidable: its counterparty universe — SOEs, government-linked entities, project vehicles in high-risk jurisdictions — demands a more robust AML/CFT framework than most commercial banks, not a lighter one. FMO has understood this for years and has invested accordingly.
03
Rolling RTS Publication Calendar
AMLA and EBA are publishing binding regulatory technical standards on a quarterly cadence through 2026–2027. Each new standard requires assessment against current policy and potential procedure revision. Without a dedicated programme function tracking these publications in real time, Invest International risks implementing a framework that is already partially outdated by the time it is finalised.
04
Strategic Context — Merger Option
The planned merger with Invest-NL (January 2028) is a further strategic consideration — though secondary to the primary AMLR compliance obligation. A fully AMLR-ready framework will significantly simplify any future harmonisation process and reduce the compliance risk profile of the combined entity. This adds to the case for a thorough, embedded programme approach rather than a minimal scoped delivery.
Section 02 — Industry Context & Enforcement Precedent

What Other Institutions Have Done — And What Happened When They Did Not

The Dutch financial sector's experience with AML enforcement over the past decade provides an unambiguous reference point. Institutions that treated AML programme investment as a cost centre rather than a strategic priority — or that allowed known gaps to persist without structured remediation — eventually faced consequences that extended far beyond regulatory fines. Rabobank, ING, and ABN AMRO have each been through this at significant cost. None of them would repeat it. The relevant question for Invest International is not whether its risk profile warrants serious investment — it self-evidently does. The question is whether to invest proactively now, or reactively later.

ING Bank N.V. (2018) — €775 million: ING's enforcement action was the result of years of known deficiencies in customer due diligence, transaction monitoring, and high-risk client management that were not addressed at programme level. The bank had identified internal gaps through audit and compliance review but failed to remediate them in a structured, timely way. The result: a deferred prosecution agreement with the Dutch Public Prosecution Service, a multi-year compulsory remediation programme run by external parties, and senior management departures. The total cost of remediation — including operational disruption, external consultants, system overhauls, and reputational damage — substantially exceeded the headline fine. ING did not want to be in this position. They are currently among the institutions most invested in AMLR readiness.

ABN AMRO (2021) — €480 million: ABN AMRO's settlement related to persistent failures in CDD processes, inadequate client risk classification, and deficient transaction monitoring — all issues that compliance functions had flagged internally over an extended period. Supervisory escalation through DNB preceded the enforcement action by several years, during which the bank's remediation efforts were deemed insufficient. The reputational damage to ABN AMRO's wholesale banking franchise — which operates in many of the same geographies as Invest International — lasted well beyond the fine itself. Multiple senior compliance and risk officers left the institution during the enforcement period.

FMO — The Direct Comparable: FMO is the most relevant reference point for Invest International. As a Dutch state-owned development finance institution with an almost identical mandate, FMO operates in the same high-risk geographies, works with the same SOE and government-linked counterparty types, and faces the same structural PEP exposure that characterises the DFI sector. What sets FMO apart is its sustained investment in a dedicated AML/CFT framework that goes significantly beyond what most commercial banks maintain. FMO's CDD/EDD policies, its PEP and SOE treatment protocols, and its transaction monitoring calibration all reflect the elevated risk profile of development finance. DNB has engaged with FMO directly on these expectations — and the bar they have set is materially higher than for a retail bank. Invest International operates in the same supervisory category. The same elevated expectations apply.

Regulatory engagement & institutional insight: The above is not generic market observation. This assessment reflects direct experience working within JST (Joint Supervisory Team) engagement frameworks and regulatory interface at Dutch institutions — including first-hand understanding of how DNB and the ECB structure their supervisory expectations for institutions with complex risk profiles. Through close professional engagement with DNB counterparts, I have been able to understand at first hand how seriously supervisors take AMLR implementation timelines at DFIs — and the level of documentation, governance evidence, and procedural completeness they expect to see at examination. This intelligence directly informs the engagement approach proposed here.

Why this is the standard, not the exception: Every institution that has been through a major AML regulatory transition — ING's post-enforcement remediation, Rabobank's transformation programme, FMO's ongoing DFI-specific framework development — has used an embedded programme manager with clear accountability. Not a document reviewer. Not a policy writer hired for a fixed deliverable. A dedicated person who owns the programme end to end, monitors the regulatory calendar, keeps stakeholders aligned, translates technical standards into operational procedures, and ensures that when the supervisory examination comes, the framework is not just documented — it is demonstrably embedded. This is not a gold-plated approach. It is what works.

The current market reality: Every major Dutch financial institution is currently investing heavily in AMLR readiness. ING, Rabobank, and ABN AMRO — having learned from costly enforcement — are treating AMLR as a Board-level programme. None of them are cutting corners. None of them are running this as a scoped document project. They understand that AFM and DNB will scrutinise implementation depth, not just the existence of an updated policy file. Invest International operates in the same supervisory environment. The bar is the same.

The regulatory scrutiny dimension: AFM and DNB consistently apply a risk-proportionate supervisory strategy — meaning institutions with the most complex risk profiles receive the most intensive oversight. A development finance institution with SOE counterparties, PEP-adjacent relationships, and project finance in high-risk jurisdictions sits at the highest end of that spectrum. DNB has made clear through supervisory dialogue and sector guidance that AMLR-readiness for such institutions requires a demonstrably robust, documented, and tested framework by 10 July 2027. A framework that is still incomplete at that date — even partially — is not a compliant framework.

What a standalone gap analysis cannot do: Option B, even executed perfectly, leaves Invest International with a comprehensive list of what needs to change and a set of revised policy documents. It does not provide: continuity of ownership across the full RTS publication cycle, translation of technical standards into operational staff procedure, training and staff readiness, management reporting for Board visibility, or the regulatory-facing documentation that demonstrates a managed, intentional implementation rather than a reactive one. Gap analysis without embedded follow-through is the most expensive form of compliance investment — because it invariably requires a second engagement to action.

Why the consultant you engage matters: AMLR implementation for a DFI is a specialist task. The person running it needs to understand not just what AMLR requires in legal terms, but how DNB and AFM approach DFI supervision, what a JST examination looks like in practice, and how regulators interpret framework quality versus framework existence. My background includes direct engagement with JST supervisory processes, regulatory interface at systemically important Dutch institutions, and a close professional relationship with a senior DNB counterpart who has communicated directly how seriously the regulator views AMLR implementation timelines at institutions with complex risk profiles. This is not theoretical. It is the operational reality of the supervisory environment Invest International is navigating.

Section 05 — Initial Discovery

Questions to Consider Before We Begin

The questions below form the basis of the initial discovery session that precedes any formal gap analysis. They are designed not to be answered in advance, but to be considered — because the degree of certainty or uncertainty with which Invest International can answer them is itself a diagnostic. Institutions that can answer most of these questions with precision and documented evidence typically face a more contained implementation challenge. Institutions that find several of these questions difficult to answer are — by definition — looking at a more substantial gap.

A note on how to use these questions: These are not a checklist — they are a structured way of surfacing what you know, what you assume, and what you genuinely do not know about your current AML/CFT position relative to AMLR requirements. The answers will determine whether Option A or Option B is the appropriate starting point, and they will shape the scope and depth of the initial gap analysis. Investing 30 minutes thinking through these before our first conversation will make that conversation substantially more productive — and may reveal that the challenge is either more manageable than expected, or more urgent.

01
Programme Governance
Who is currently accountable for your AML/CFT compliance programme — and do they have a dedicated mandate, or is it combined with other responsibilities?
Most DFIs at Invest International's scale that have faced regulatory attention had diffuse or shared AML ownership. AMLR requires a demonstrable, named accountability structure. Shared ownership typically means no ownership when it matters.
▲ High Impact
02
AMLR Gap Awareness
Has anyone in your compliance function done a structured comparison of your current WWFT-based framework against AMLR's specific requirements — and if so, what were the findings?
Most institutions have done informal assessments or read summary guides. Very few have a structured, documented gap register. The difference matters enormously: an informed gap register allows prioritisation; an informal impression does not.
★ Critical
03
CDD / EDD Design
How are your current CDD and EDD procedures designed to handle counterparties that are simultaneously a government entity, a borrower, and a PEP-adjacent relationship — and are those procedures documented?
This is the single most common gap in DFI compliance frameworks. The AMLR has materially stricter EDD triggers for this exact counterparty profile. If the answer to "are those procedures documented" is no or partial, that is where the gap analysis will begin.
★ Critical
04
Risk Classification
When were your client risk classifications last reviewed — and does your current methodology account for the structural PEP and SOE exposure that is inherent to your mandate, or does it treat those as exceptions?
Many DFIs' risk classification models were designed for commercial banking and retrofitted. AMLR requires that PEP and SOE exposure be treated as a structural design parameter, not an exception flag. A methodology that was not built for this will require rebuilding, not adjusting.
▲ High Impact
05
Transaction Monitoring
Has your transaction monitoring system been calibrated for development finance transaction profiles — long disbursement cycles, multi-tranche project structures, government-to-project vehicle flows — or does it use parameters derived from commercial bank benchmarks?
TM systems calibrated for commercial banking generate significant false positives and false negatives when applied to DFI transaction profiles. AMLR's ITS on TM calibration will require documented evidence of a fit-for-purpose model. This is frequently a significant remediation item.
▲ High Impact
06
UBO & Registry Coverage
For your current portfolio of counterparties in jurisdictions with limited or non-existent UBO registry infrastructure — what is your documented procedure for achieving AMLR-standard beneficial ownership verification?
AMLR significantly raises the evidential bar for UBO verification in third-country contexts. DFIs operating in emerging markets, frontier economies, or conflict-affected states will face this challenge across a significant portion of their portfolio. Informal practices will not survive supervisory examination.
★ Critical
07
Board Visibility
Does your Board currently receive structured, regular reporting on the status of your AMLR readiness — and has AMLR implementation been formally added to your compliance programme governance calendar?
DNB expects Board-level visibility on AMLR preparation at all institutions in the current window. Absence of documented Board engagement on AMLR readiness is itself a finding in supervisory examinations. This is not bureaucracy — it is a governance signal that regulators look for.
● Governance
08
RTS Tracking
Is someone at Invest International currently monitoring the quarterly AMLA and EBA technical standard publications, assessing their relevance to your framework, and documenting the response decisions?
There are over 40 regulatory technical and implementing standards expected between now and July 2027. Each one has the potential to require policy or procedure revision. Without a dedicated tracking function, institutions typically discover the cumulative impact of these standards too late to respond proportionately.
● Technical
09
Sanctions & Screening
How are you currently screening counterparties, beneficial owners, and project sponsors against EU, UN, US (OFAC), and UK sanctions lists — and how is that process documented and evidenced for regulatory review?
Sanctions screening for DFIs is materially more complex than for commercial banks. Project finance structures, SPVs, and government-linked entities create screening challenges that off-the-shelf tools frequently fail to address. AMLR and the current geopolitical environment make this a priority examination area for DNB.
★ Critical
10
Legacy File Quality
What percentage of your active client portfolio has CDD documentation that would meet AMLR's evidential standards today — and when was that documentation last independently tested?
Legacy file quality is typically the most time-consuming remediation item in any AML transformation. Institutions that have not conducted a recent, independent file quality review frequently discover — too late — that a material proportion of their portfolio requires re-KYC at significant cost and client friction. AMLR's enhanced documentation requirements make this exposure worse, not better.
▲ High Impact
11
Staff Readiness
Do the people executing CDD and EDD at Invest International understand AMLR's requirements well enough to apply them to your specific counterparty profiles — and is that understanding tested and documented?
Policy without trained staff is a document, not a control. AMLR examinations look for evidence of operational embedding — not just procedural documentation. A gap between what the policy says and what front-line staff do is one of the most common findings in Dutch AML enforcement actions.
● Operational
12
Strategic Question
If DNB requested an inspection of your AMLR readiness tomorrow — not in July 2027, but tomorrow — what would you show them, and how confident would you be that it demonstrates a managed, intentional programme?
This is the question that matters. DNB does not announce inspections in advance. Institutions that are only compliant on the deadline date are, in practice, running a significant risk. The answer to this question will tell you more about your actual AMLR exposure than any formal gap analysis framework.
★ Critical

How to use these questions: You don't need to have answers before we speak. Identifying which questions you cannot answer with confidence — and which answers you would need to verify before committing — is itself the starting point for a productive initial discovery conversation. A good AMLR engagement begins with an honest assessment of the current state; these questions are designed to make that assessment structured and efficient. Bring whatever documentation you have and whatever uncertainty you're carrying — both are useful.
